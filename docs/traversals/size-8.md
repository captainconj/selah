## Basin of אויב — "enemy"

**GV = 19.** Letters: א ו י ב — aleph, vav, yod, bet.

| Word | Meaning | → |
|------|---------|---|
| **אויב** | **enemy** | **FIXED** |
| ואבי | and-my-father | → אויב |
| אביו | his-father | → אויב |
| ויבא | and-he-came | → אויב |
| יובא | he-was-brought | → אויב |
| יבאו | they-will-come | → אויב |
| יבוא | he-will-come | → אויב |
| ובאי | and-my-coming | → אויב |

**Interpretation.**

The basin of ENEMY contains "his father" and every form of "come." This is the basin of family betrayal.

אביו (his father) walks to אויב (enemy). The most intimate relationship becomes opposition. Not a stranger — a father. Joseph's brothers. Absalom and David. Isaac binding his son. The father is the original enemy because he is the one you cannot ignore.

And every form of coming — ויבא (and he came), יבוא (he will come), יבאו (they will come) — all arrive at enemy. To come is to confront. Every arrival is an encounter with opposition. There is no coming that does not meet resistance. The Hebrew word for enemy contains the Hebrew word for father (אב) and the Hebrew letters of coming (בוא). They were always the same word.

## Basin of ימות — "he will die"

**GV = 456.** Letters: י מ ו ת — yod, mem, vav, tav.

| Word | Meaning | → |
|------|---------|---|
| מותי | my-death | → ימות |
| וימת | and-he-died | → ימות |
| **ימות** | **he-will-die** | **FIXED** |
| יומת | he-shall-be-put-to-death | → ימות |
| ומתי | and-when / and-my-dead | → ימות |
| ימתו | they-will-die | → ימות |
| מתיו | Matthew (Mattityahu) | → ימות |
| יתמו | they-will-be-complete / they-will-be-finished | → ימות |

**Interpretation.**

The basin of DEATH contains Matthew.

מתיו (Matthew, from מתתיהו — "gift of YHWH") shares letters with ימות (he will die). The gift of God walks to death. The gospel-writer's name is an anagram of dying. He wrote the death, and his name IS the death.

Every form of dying flows here: my death (מותי), he died (וימת), he shall be executed (יומת), they will die (ימתו). And then: יתמו (they will be completed / they will be finished). Completion and death share the same basin. To finish is to die. To die is to be finished. The Hebrew תמם means both "to be complete" and "to be consumed."

And ומתי — "and when." The question of timing (when?) shares letters with death. Every "when" is already counting toward an end.
